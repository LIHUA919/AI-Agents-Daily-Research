### [52] [AGNT2: Autonomous Agent Economies on Interaction-Optimized Layer 2 Infrastructure](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21129)
*Anbang Ruan,Xing Zhang*

Main category: cs.MA

TL;DR: AGNT2 is a purpose-built blockchain Layer 2 stack designed specifically for AI agent coordination, addressing the limitations of current L2 solutions that are optimized for human financial transactions rather than high-frequency agent interactions.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Current blockchain Layer 2 solutions (Optimism, Arbitrum, zkSync) are designed for human-initiated financial transactions, but autonomous AI agents generate high-frequency, semantically rich service invocations among untrusting parties. Existing chains treat agent interactions as generic calldata, forcing identity, escrow, dependency ordering, and session state to be encoded above the execution layer inefficiently.

Method: AGNT2 presents a three-tier stack: (1) sidecar deployment pattern to turn Docker containers into on-chain agents without code modification, (2) Layer Top P2P state channels for bilateral pairs (<100 ms, 1K-5K TPS per pair), Layer Core as dependency-aware sequenced rollup for multi-party interactions (500 ms-2 s, 300K-500K TPS), Layer Root settlement with computational fraud proofs anchored to EVM L1, and (3) agent-native execution environment with interaction trie making service invocation, identity, reputation, capabilities, and session context first-class protocol objects.

Result: Simulation and analytical modeling support the architecture, and prototype measurements validate selected components, but no end-to-end Layer Core implementation exists yet. Practical deployment is currently constrained to roughly 10K-100K TPS by data availability throughput, leaving a ~100x gap at the target ceiling.

Conclusion: AGNT2 argues that the agent economy requires a dedicated execution layer rather than repurposing general-purpose chains for agents, focusing on solving the execution-layer systems problems of sequencing, state, settlement, and the data-availability bandwidth gap.

Abstract: Current blockchain Layer 2 solutions, including Optimism, Arbitrum, zkSync, and their derivatives, optimize for human-initiated financial transactions. Autonomous AI agents instead generate high-frequency, semantically rich service invocations among mutually untrusting principals. Existing chains treat those interactions as generic calldata, forcing identity, escrow, dependency ordering, and session state to be encoded above the execution layer at the wrong cost point. We present AGNT2, a three-tier stack purpose-built for agent and microservice coordination on-chain. AGNT2 combines: (1) a sidecar deployment pattern that turns any Docker container into an on-chain agent without application-code modification; (2) Layer Top P2P state channels for established bilateral pairs (<100 ms, rough design target 1K-5K TPS per pair, 10M+ aggregate TPS design envelope under endpoint-resource limits), Layer Core as a dependency-aware sequenced rollup for first-contact and multi-party interactions (500 ms-2 s, 300K-500K TPS design target), and Layer Root settlement with computational fraud proofs anchored to any EVM L1; and (3) an agent-native execution environment plus interaction trie that make service invocation, identity, reputation, capabilities, and session context first-class protocol objects. This paper focuses on the execution-layer systems problem: sequencing, state, settlement, and the data-availability (DA) bandwidth gap that bounds all three. Simulation and analytical modeling support the architecture, and prototype measurements validate selected components, but no end-to-end Layer Core implementation exists yet. Practical deployment is currently constrained to roughly 10K-100K TPS by DA throughput, leaving a ~100x gap at the target ceiling. AGNT2 argues that the agent economy requires a dedicated execution layer rather than a general-purpose chain repurposed for agents.

### [53] [Role of diversity in team performance: the case of missing expertise, an agent based simulation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21328)
*Tamás Kiss*

Main category: cs.MA

TL;DR: An agent-based simulation study examining how functional diversity composition in management teams affects communication and performance, revealing context-dependent effects of intrapersonal functional diversity and dominant function diversity, and identifying a need for a third measure capturing aggregate team expertise.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Existing research on management teams often focuses on limited modes of variable distributions and struggles to analyze contextual effects. The abstract specifically cites functional diversity research where management teams are described by complex distributions of capabilities, making it difficult to investigate hidden distributions and their effects on team communication and performance.

Method: The researchers developed an agent-based model and conducted a series of simulation experiments to investigate the effects of functional diversity composition on team communication and performance, allowing them to examine hidden distributions and contextual factors that are difficult to study empirically.

Result: Simulation results show that intrapersonal functional diversity (IFD) and dominant function diversity (DFD) can either enhance or reduce performance and communication depending on context (such as communication schemes among agents or their functional composition). Additionally, the results suggest that a third measure capturing the aggregate expertise of the team is needed alongside IFD and DFD to comprehensively account for empirical findings.

Conclusion: The effects of functional diversity in management teams are context-dependent, and existing diversity measures (IFD and DFD) need to be complemented by a third measure that captures aggregate team expertise to fully explain team performance and communication patterns in empirical research.

Abstract: Theory and empirical research on management teams' influence on firm performance have witnessed continuous development, and by now incorporate numerous details. Classic, experiment-based studies examining social systems collect vast amount of data, but often times investigate only the first one or two modes of the distribution of measured variables, and experience difficulty in analyzing the effect of context. For example, in functional diversity research, management teams are described by measures incorporating complex distributions of capabilities of individual managers and teams of managers. To investigate the effect of hidden distributions, and the effect of functional diversity composition on team communication and performance, we developed an agent-based model, and conducted a series of simulation experiments. Modeling results show that depending on the context, such as communication scheme among interacting agents, or their functional composition, intrapersonal functional diversity (IFD), and dominant function diversity (DFD) might enhance or reduce performance and communication among agents. Furthermore, simulation results also suggest that a third measure is required alongside IFD and DFD capturing the aggregate expertise of the team to comprehensively account for empirical findings.

### [54] [Architectures for Robust Self-Organizing Energy Systems under Information and Control Constraints](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21529)
*Emilie Frost,Astrid Nieße*

Main category: cs.MA

TL;DR: Controlled self-organization in agent-based CPES with observer/controller architectures enables robustness against cyber attacks while respecting privacy, regulatory, and data exchange constraints.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Cyber attacks pose major threats to current energy systems, and while controlled self-organization through observer/controller architectures can enhance robustness, implementation must consider privacy of local distributed energy resources, regulatory constraints, and data exchange requirements.

Method: Proposes architecture variants for observer and controller that account for restrictions on information access and limited actions, and evaluates possible controller actions across different architectures.

Result: The evaluation underscores the importance of considering observer/controller architectures in agent-based system design to ensure robustness for real-world applications.

Conclusion: Observer/controller architectures must be carefully designed to balance controlled self-organization capabilities with practical constraints like privacy and regulatory limitations for effective implementation in CPES.

Abstract: Applying the concept of controlled self-organization in agent-based Cyber-Physical Energy Systems (CPES) is a promising approach to ensure system robustness. By introducing an observer/controller architecture to the system, this concept allows for self-organization while still enabling intervention when disturbances occur. Thus, it is possible to respond to effects of cyber attacks, a major threat to current energy systems. However, when implementing an observer to monitor the system and a controller to execute actions for controlled self-organization in CPES, it is essential to take into account restrictions on information and actions resulting from the privacy of local distributed energy resources, regulatory constraints, and data exchange requirements. For this reason, this paper presents architecture variants for the observer and controller that take into account restrictions on access to information and limited actions. In addition, it evaluates possible controller actions in various architectures. The results underscore the importance of considering observer/controller architectures when designing agent-based systems to ensure their robustness for real-world applications.

### [55] [Agentic AI-Enabled Framework for Thermal Comfort and Building Energy Assessment in Tropical Urban Neighborhoods](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21787)
*Po-Yen Lai,Xinyu Yang,Derrick Low,Huizhe Liu,Jian Cheng Wong*

Main category: cs.MA

TL;DR: An agentic AI framework combining LLMs with lightweight physics models for automated evaluation of urban cooling strategies and thermal comfort in Singapore.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To address urban heat island effects and building energy demands in Singapore through an automated system that can evaluate climate-resilient urban design strategies.

Method: Integrates large language models (LLMs) with lightweight physics-based models through prompt customization. LLMs interpret urban design tasks, extract policies, and activate appropriate physics models that use core thermal/airflow principles to predict microclimate variables and estimate thermal comfort indices and energy usage.

Result: The framework enables exploration of various climate-resilient strategies (green façades, cool paint) to improve thermal comfort while reducing wall heat gain and energy demand. Demonstrates potential for cross-disciplinary applications in sustainable urban design.

Conclusion: By combining LLMs' autonomous reasoning with rapid quantitative evaluation of lightweight physics models, the system shows promise for sustainable urban design, indoor-outdoor environmental integration, and climate adaptation planning.

Abstract: In response to the urban heat island effects and building energy demands in Singapore, this study proposes an agentic AI-enabled reasoning framework that integrates large language models (LLMs) with lightweight physics-based models. Through prompt customization, the LLMs interpret urban design tasks, extract relevant policies, and activate appropriate physics-based models for evaluation, forming a closed-loop reasoning-action process. These lightweight physics-based models leverage core thermal and airflow principles, streamlining conventional models to reduce computational time while predicting microclimate variables, such as building surface temperature, ground radiant heat, and airflow conditions, thereby enabling the estimation of thermal comfort indices, e.g., physiological equivalent temperature (PET), and building energy usage. This framework allows users to explore a variety of climate-resilient building surface strategies, e.g., green façades and cool paint applications, that improve thermal comfort while reducing wall heat gain and energy demand. By combining the autonomous reasoning capacity of LLMs with the rapid quantitative evaluation of lightweight physics-based models, the proposed system demonstrates potential for cross-disciplinary applications in sustainable urban design, indoor-outdoor environmental integration, and climate adaptation planning. The source code and data used in this study are available at: https://github.com/PgUpDn/urban-cooling-agent.
